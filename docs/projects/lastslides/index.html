There are four types of technologies

  • Emerging
  • Sustaining
  • Converging
  • Disruptive

Emerging technologies are unquie underdeveloped prototypes from anybody.

Sustaining technology are already established technologies which continue to be developed and used.

Converging technologies are sustaining technologies which are similar enough and tend to merge.

Disruptive technologies are emerging technologies that displace an existing norm.

The Engineering side of Sustainable Development

Analysis should be holistic and include environmental impact.

Local stakeholders and cultures should be involved.

All costs should be included in the price of the good/service.

Sustainable technologies are:

  • Technically feasible
  • Ecologically sound
  • Economically viable
  • Socially responsible

The 12 Green Engineering Principles

Principle 1

non-hazardous

Strive to ensure that all material and energy inputs and outputs are as inherently non-hazardous as possible.

Principle 2

prevent waste

It is better to prevent waste than to treat or clean up waste after it is formed.

Principle 3

separate and purify

Separation and purification operations should be a component of the design framework.

Principle 4

maximize efficiency

System components should be designed to maximize mass, energy and temporal efficiency.

Principle 5

pull not push

System components should be output pulled rather than input pushed through the use of energy and materials.

Principle 6

entropy & complexity

Embedded entropy and complexity must be viewed as an investment when making design choices on recycle, reuse or beneficial disposition.

Principle 7

durable

Targeted durability, not immortality, should be a design goal.

Principle 8

big things fall hard

Design for unnecessary capacity or capability should be considered a design flaw. This includes engineering “one size fits all” solutions.

Principle 9

diversity is a double edged sword

Multi-component products should strive for material unification to promote disassembly and value retention. (minimize material diversity)

Principle 10

intergate

Design of processes and systems must include integration and interconnectivity with available energy and materials flows.

Principle 11

unseen costs

Performance metrics include designing for performance in commercial “after-life”.

Principle 12

the problem of the commons

Design should be based on renewable and readily available inputs.

Digital Twins

Definition: A digital replica of a physical thing. Furthermore, connection between the digital and physical thing is important as well as generating real-time data using sensors.

Digital Shadows/Footprint

Is the set of collected digital data associated with a particular thing. In the case of a footprint, one usually is refering to a person.

Digital Shadows/Footprint

Example: A persons browsing history and cookies.

Cyber Physical System

Is a symbiotic relationship between a digital system and a physical one. For example, buses real-time tracking for the departure boards.

Additive and subtractive manufacturing

Manufacturing is a fancy way of saying putting stuff together.
Subtractive manufacturing is all about controlling the process of removing material.
Additive manufacturing by contrast is controlling the process of adding material.

Additive Manufacturing

There are common types of fillament (raw material): metals, ceramics and plastics.

Reactions include: Thermal (melting/solidifying), chemical (glue/adhesive) and electromagnetic (projected light, lasers, electron beam).

Additive Manufacturing

Common methods:

  • Fused Deposition modelling (FDM/FFF)
  • Inkjet printing
  • Powder bed printing
  • Stereolithography (SLA/SL)
  • Laminated Object
  • Selective Laser Sintering (SLS/SLM)

Additive Manufacturing

Each additive method/combination can be charaterized by:

  • structure - infill?
  • properties - brittle?, speed?
  • processing - extra steps before having the finished object
  • performance - how well it performs in the desired task/role

Suppose you have a recurrence relation:
T(n) = a*T(n/b)+ f(n^c)

T(n) = a*T(n/b)+ f(n^c)

Case 1: a > b^c
then T(n) = O(n^(log_b(a)))

T(n) = a*T(n/b)+ f(n^c)

Case 2: a = b^c
then T(n) = O(n^c * log(n))

T(n) = a*T(n/b)+ f(n^c)

Case 3: a < b^c
then T(n) = O(n^c)
